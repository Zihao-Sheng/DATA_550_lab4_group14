Our analysis reveals a fascinating story about how languages balance structure and speed to achieve similar communication efficiency. Plot A shows that languages with higher information density use fewer distinct syllables, while languages with lower information density employ larger syllable inventories. This pattern clusters by continent, which makes us believe that cultural differences have an impact on this. Plot B shows that speech rate inversely correlates with information density. Languages with simpler syllables are spoken faster, while those with complex syllables are spoken more slowly, suggesting speakers adjust their tempo based on syllable complexity. Finally, Plot C synthesizes these findings by calculating actual information rates. Despite vastly different strategies in structure and speed, all languages cluster around 35-45 bits per second. This convergence demonstrates a universal human cognitive constraint on information processingregardless of whether a language uses complex syllables spoken slowly or simple syllables spoken quickly, we all communicate at fundamentally the same rate, highlighting an inherent limit in human communication capacity.

Story: How Much Do Speakers Vary Within Their Own Language?

In the main analysis, we found that all languages cluster around similar information rates, suggesting a universal cognitive constraint. But this narrative assumes all speakers within a language speak similarly. This raises the question of how much individual variation exists within each language community compared to the differences between languages? I grouped by speaker and calculated their mean speech rate across all texts they read. This approach better reveals individual differences that might be masked when pooling all observations.

Figure 1: Speaker-Level Speech Rate Variation by Language

The first visualization shows the distribution of speaker-level mean speech rates for each language. Rather than just seeing the overall mean and confidence interval, we can now see the spread of individual speakers. This visualization reveals that while languages do cluster differently, individual speakers within the same language vary considerably. Some speakers are significantly faster or slower than others, suggesting that factors beyond language structuresuch as age, personality, or speaking styleinfluence speech rate.

Figure 2: Information Rate at the Speaker Level

Since information rate combines both speech rate and information density, examining speaker-level information rates shows whether linguistic constraints really do keep speakers uniform in their actual information transmission. Interestingly, the information rate plot shows less variation than the speech rate plot. This suggests that the linguistic constraint we discovered earlierthat fast-speaking languages have low information density while slow-speaking languages have high information density. Individual speakers naturally modulate their speech rate based on the structure of their language, keeping their information transmission remarkably consistent. A speaker might deviate from their language group's typical speech rate, but they're unconsciously compensating through their language's information density.

Figure 3: Beyond Language StructureThe Role of Speaker Demographics

But are all speakers within a language truly equivalent? The main analysis pooled all speakers together, but speakers vary in sex and age. Do these demographic factors explain some of the within-language variation we observe? This comparison reveals a consistent pattern. Across nearly every language and continent, male speakers tend to speak slightly faster than female speakers. This demographic effect is small but systematic, and it persists even after controlling for language structure. The consistency of this pattern across diverse linguistic families suggests it reflects biological or social factors that transcend language boundaries.

Conclusion

By shifting from text-level to speaker-level analysis, we've uncovered a richer picture of human speech variation. While the original analysis showed that all languages achieve similar information rates, we now see that individual speakers introduce considerable variation around these linguistic averages. Demographic factors like sex appear to systematically influence speech rate. Yet, speakers seem to naturally adjust their speech to maintain relatively stable information transmission rates, suggesting that the human cognitive limit on information processing is truly fundamental, not just at the language level, but reflected in the behavior of individual speakers across the world.
